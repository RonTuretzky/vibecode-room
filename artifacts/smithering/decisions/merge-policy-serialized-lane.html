Merge policy: a serialized merge lane, not optimistic eviction

ORCH-A-02   Panopticon V0 implementation orchestration  ·  docs/planning/06-orchestration.md §2

The problem (and the trap)

Smithers' MergeQueue component is only a concurrency limiter — it serializes who writes, nothing more. The seductive design is optimistic merging with postsubmit eviction: land each ticket the moment its own gates are green, run the full suite on main afterward, and if something breaks, automatically evict the culprit. But that "automatic eviction" is entirely custom logic we would have to build and verify ourselves: a CheckSuite to attribute the postsubmit failure, a Saga to compensate every dependent run that already branched off the bad commit, a jj revert to surgically back the change out, and a re-rebase of everyone downstream (where Smithers worktree runs auto-rebase on resume even if the rebase fails, materializing conflicts that a human or agent must then resolve).

Decision

For V0 we build a serialized merge lane and we do not build optimistic merging or postsubmit auto-eviction.

Why a serialized lane is sufficient here

  • The DAG already serializes the hard part. Dependents cannot start until their ancestors land, so the lane mostly sees independent tickets whose rebases are trivial. The widest ready-set is ~6 (Phase-0 fan-out / Phase-1 probes); the lane drains them one at a time in seconds.
  • Each land is already pre-merge-green with recorded red-before-green. A ticket reaches the lane only after its deterministic gate tier passed on its rebased tip (see ORCH-A-03). Postsubmit failures are therefore expected to be rare — not the common case eviction is designed for.
  • Eviction's correctness bar is brutal. To auto-evict safely you must prove attribution (which of N optimistically-landed commits broke postsubmit?), compensate every Saga branch that already forked, and survive cascade rebases — each a place to silently lose work, which REQ-15's durability ethic forbids. Building that to save a handful of serialized lands is a bad trade.

Alternatives

Optimistic merge + automatic postsubmit eviction. Rejected for V0. High custom-logic surface (CheckSuite + Saga + jj revert + cascade re-rebase) for low payoff at this ticket count; its own failure modes threaten silent work loss.
Trunk-based, everyone commits to main, no gate. Rejected. Violates the validation bar — a ticket could land without recorded RBG.
Depth-1 serialized lane, pre-merge gate on rebased tip, postsubmit full suite, manual revert (chosen). Chosen. Uses MergeQueue for exactly what it is, keeps every land gated and reversible, and defers the expensive eviction machinery until a future build's scale actually justifies it.

Verification (the orchestration logic is itself tested)

  • Lane-serialization test: enqueue two ticket lands concurrently → assert exactly one holds the write lock at a time and the second rebases onto the first's result. RBG: set lane depth to 2 → both write → interleave detector fails.
  • Gate-before-land test: a ticket with a missing/failing pre-merge gate or no recorded RBG cannot enter the lane. RBG: stub a ticket with rbgRecorded:false → assert land is refused; flip to true with both red+green logs present → land allowed.
  • Rebase-conflict bounce test: force a jj conflict on rebase → assert the ticket is returned to its worker with the conflict materialized (not force-landed). RBG: auto-resolve-by-discard → conflict-bounce assertion fails.
  • Postsubmit-revert test: inject a postsubmit failure on main → assert a revert-or-forward-fix decision row is written to artifacts/smithering/decisions/build/ and no autonomous eviction fires.
Carried risk: serialized lands are slower under a hypothetical wide, churny DAG. Mitigation: the DAG is verified narrow (max antichain ~6) and dependency-bound; if a future build is merge-bound rather than dependency-bound, optimistic eviction becomes worth its cost and is revisited at the gate — not before.

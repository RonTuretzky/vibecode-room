Merge policy: optimistic merge + postsubmit eviction

ORCH-A-02   Vibersyn V0 implementation orchestration  ·  docs/planning/06-orchestration.md §2
Reversed. The prior decision built a serialized merge lane (depth-1, one writer to main at a time) and explicitly forbade optimistic merging and postsubmit eviction. That is overturned. We now prefer optimistic merging — land-then-learn with postsubmit eviction — because it is faster. We do build the optimistic land + postsubmit eviction machinery that was previously declared out of scope.

The decision

For V0 we build optimistic merging with postsubmit eviction. Speed is the priority: a ticket lands the moment its own gates are green — it does not wait behind a serialized lane — and the full suite runs on main afterward. If a postsubmit run goes red, the culprit is automatically evicted (backed out) and downstream is re-rebased.

Why optimistic is the right trade now

  • Speed is the priority. Build this as fast as possible. Serializing every land behind a single writer throttles a wide, mostly-independent ready-set; optimistic land lets the whole antichain land in parallel.
  • Conflicts are kept rare by ordering. Tickets are ordered to minimize merge conflicts (ORCH-A-01), so most optimistic lands are independent and clean; the rare bad land is the exception eviction exists to handle.
  • jj makes eviction tractable. Co-located jj+git gives first-class conflict handling and jj revert as a clean back-out primitive, so attribute-and-evict is a recoverable operation rather than a corrupt-history hazard.

The flow

ticket gates green  →  LAND optimistically on main  (no lane wait)
   └─ postsubmit full suite runs on the new main tip
         ├─ green  →  done
         └─ red    →  CheckSuite attributes the failure → jj revert the culprit (EVICT)
                       → re-rebase downstream → re-run their gates

Alternatives

Serialized depth-1 merge lane, no eviction (prior plan). Reversed. Slower — queues lands behind one writer; throttles a wide independent ready-set. The whole point of this reversal is speed.
Trunk-based, everyone commits to main, no gate. Rejected. Violates the validation bar — a ticket could land without recorded RBG.
Optimistic land on own green gates + postsubmit full suite + automatic eviction (chosen). Chosen. Fastest; every land still gated with recorded RBG; bad lands are auto-evicted and downstream re-rebased.

Verification (the orchestration logic is itself tested)

  • Optimistic-land test: a ticket whose own gates are green lands immediately without waiting on another in-flight land. RBG: force a serialized single-writer lock → assert the parallel land is not blocked (else the optimistic-land assertion fails).
  • Gate-before-land test: a ticket with a missing/failing gate or no recorded RBG cannot land. RBG: stub a ticket with rbgRecorded:false → assert land is refused; flip to true with both red+green logs present → land allowed.
  • Postsubmit-eviction test: inject a postsubmit failure on main → assert the offending land is attributed and jj revert-evicted automatically, and downstream is re-rebased. RBG: disable attribution → eviction targets the wrong commit → fails.
  • Cascade-rebase test: after an eviction, dependents that branched off the bad commit are re-rebased and re-gated. RBG: skip the cascade → a dependent keeps the reverted change → fails.
Carried risk: optimistic land + eviction is custom logic (attribution, jj revert, cascade re-rebase) with its own failure modes. Mitigation: conflict-minimizing ticket order keeps bad lands rare; every eviction is recorded (artifacts/smithering/decisions/build/); jj's first-class conflict handling makes the back-out recoverable. The speed win outweighs the machinery's cost at this build's scale.

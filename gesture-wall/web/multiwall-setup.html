Gesture Wall · Deployment Guide

Two connected walls, three cameras

A concrete build: two adjacent walls that meet at a corner and act as one continuous surface, lit by two edge-aligned projectors, sensed by three cameras, all driven by one host.

The room, fixed for this guide

  • Wall A + Wall B join at a seam → one continuous coordinate space
  • Projector 1 → Wall A, Projector 2 → Wall B, butted/blended at the seam
  • cam0 · cam1 · cam2 — sides + corner, on one sensing host
camera (sensing) wall (display) sensing server (fusion)

Why this shape → a corner puts two big targets inside one arm-sweep of a user who barely moves, and the seam gives the cameras a natural overlap to hand off through.

The one idea that makes it scale

Separate sensing from display

Today the app fuses them: one camera, one wall, one process. To grow, split them into two layers glued by a calibration:

  • Cameras produce pointer observations — a wrist position in image coordinates, plus “engaged”.
  • Walls are display surfaces with a zone grid + dwell selector, in wall coordinates.
  • The glue is a homography Hcam→wall — exactly the 4-corner calibration you already have, one per camera/wall pair.

A camera never “owns” a wall. It just reports where a hand is; a fusion step decides which wall that means and routes it there.

Why split them → you can add, move, or recalibrate one camera or wall without touching the rest — only its homography changes. Glued together, every change is a rebuild.

Three coordinate frames

What every number means

image  [0,1]² per camera. Where MediaPipe reports the wrist in that camera’s frame. Different for every camera.
wall  [0,1]² per wall. Where the cursor lives on a projected surface; zones are defined here. Resolution-independent.
room  optional shared frame. If cameras overlap, map each into one room frame so you can match the same person seen by two cameras.

Because the walls are connected, calibrate so they form one continuous space: Wall A’s right edge (x=1) and Wall B’s left edge (x=0) meet at the seam. You need a homography for each camera/wall pair it can cover:

cam0 → wallA
cam1 → wallA        (cam1 sees the corner seam)
cam1 → wallB
cam2 → wallB

4 homographies — not 6. cam0 never covers Wall B, cam2 never covers Wall A. A cursor crossing the seam hands off cam0→cam2 via cam1.

Why three frames → separating image from wall keeps every camera’s calibration independent and the walls resolution-independent; the optional room frame is only needed to recognise the same person in two overlapping cameras.

Step 1 · The connected walls

One continuous surface, two clients

  • Run two wall clients (the web app, fullscreen), one per projector — but calibrated so they’re continuous at the seam: A’s right edge = B’s left edge.
  • Both walls run the same grid (2×3) + their own dwell state — tiles can sit right up to the seam since the surfaces abut.
  • A wall doesn’t read a camera. It receives a cursor {x,y,engaged} in its own coords; the server decides A vs B and hands off across the seam.
  • Drive both from one host via an extended desktop — projector 1 on display 1, projector 2 on display 2.

Tiny change to the current app: add a “remote pointer” source that takes {x,y,engaged} off a WebSocket instead of the local webcam.

Why calibrate them continuous → if A’s right edge and B’s left edge line up, a target on the corner reads as one surface and the cursor doesn’t teleport at the seam — users never sense it’s two systems.

Step 1b · Make the seam invisible

Align the two projectors

  • Each projector faces its own wall. It sits out in the room in front of that face, firing square onto it — ceiling-mounted short-throw is cleanest, so the user doesn’t cast a shadow. The two beams meet at the corner.
  • Extended desktop, not mirrored. Each projector is its own display; place display 2 immediately right of display 1 so the OS coordinate space is continuous.
  • Butt or overlap at the seam. Either edge-to-edge (simplest) or a small overlap with edge-blend so there’s no bright line and no gap where tiles would fall through.
  • Match brightness & keystone on both so a tile straddling the seam reads as one tile.
  • Then calibrate cameras to the projected image — corners are calibrated to what you actually see on the wall, so projector alignment must be locked first.

Order matters: geometry → projector alignment → camera calibration → run. Re-aligning a projector later means re-calibrating that wall’s cameras.

Why align first → cameras are calibrated to the projected corners. If the image shifts, every camera mapping is wrong; and a bright seam line or a gap would split a corner tile so it can’t be pointed at cleanly.

Step 2 · The three cameras

Sides for reach, center for the seam

Like the single-cam app, each camera sits at its wall and faces the user (not the wall). The user stands in the pocket facing the corner:

  • cam0 — mounted on Wall A, looking back across the pocket at the user so A’s far edge isn’t a steep oblique angle.
  • cam2 — mirror of cam0 on Wall B.
  • cam1 — at the corner/seam, looking straight out at the user: sees both inner edges, owns the handoff, and is the occlusion fallback.

This fixes the prototype’s weak spot — a lone oblique camera makes the far side imprecise — by giving each wall’s far region a square-on second viewpoint.

Why face the user → pose tracking needs a frontal view of the body and the raised arm; a camera aimed at the wall would only see the user’s back. Side + corner placement keeps every region near square-on (accurate pointing) and means a body that hides itself from one camera is still seen by another.

Step 3 · The fusion layer

From 3 observations to 1 cursor per wall

Every frame, each camera yields a wrist + engaged. The server turns that into at most one cursor per wall:

  1. Map: push each camera’s wrist through Hcam→wall for every wall it serves.
  2. Gate: drop observations that land out of [0,1]² (not pointing at that wall).
  3. Seam handoff: near the seam a hand maps in-bounds on both A and B. Commit to a wall only past a small margin, and add hysteresis so the cursor doesn’t flicker A↔B right at the join.
  4. Fuse per wall: if two cameras see the same wall, combine — pick highest landmark confidence, or average if they agree within a few cm.

Result the wall client receives: {wall:"A", x, y, engaged}. Smoothing & dwell stay on the client, unchanged.

Why fuse centrally → collapsing many camera views into one cursor per wall is the only place that can resolve disagreement — without it, two views of the same hand would fight and the seam would flicker between walls.

Step 4 · How it actually runs

Sensing server + wall clients

The browser can’t cleanly open 3 cameras and fullscreen 2 displays at once. So split the process:

  • Sensing server — Python, reuses your PoseSource once per camera. Reads cam0/1/2, runs pose, applies homographies, fuses, and emits cursor events over WebSocket.
  • Wall clients — two browser tabs of the existing web app, each fullscreen on a projector, each subscribed to its wall’s events.

Keep filter + dwell + render on the client (already built) so the server stays a thin sensing/fusion service. Latency target: pose at ~30 fps per camera, events pushed as produced.

Why a server + clients → a browser tab can’t open three cameras and own two fullscreen displays at once. One server centralises all sensing and fusion; the walls stay the unmodified web app, just fed pointers — less code, lower latency, one place to debug.

Step 5 · One file describes the room

The config that ties it together

{
  "walls": {
    "A": { "display": 1, "grid": {"rows":2,"cols":3} },
    "B": { "display": 2, "grid": {"rows":2,"cols":3} }
  },
  "adjacency": [
    { "left": "A", "right": "B", "seam_margin": 0.06 }
  ],
  "cameras": {
    "cam0": { "device": 0, "serves": ["A"] },
    "cam1": { "device": 1, "serves": ["A","B"] },
    "cam2": { "device": 2, "serves": ["B"] }
  },
  "calibration": {
    "cam0->A": { "matrix": [[…],[…],[…]] },
    "cam1->A": { "matrix": [[…],[…],[…]] },
    "cam1->B": { "matrix": [[…],[…],[…]] },
    "cam2->B": { "matrix": [[…],[…],[…]] }
  },
  "fusion": "highest_confidence",
  "server": { "ws_port": 8770 }
}

Each matrix is exactly what the 4-corner calibration already produces — now stored per camera/wall pair instead of one global file.

  • adjacency declares the seam (A right ↔ B left) and the seam_margin the handoff hysteresis uses.
  • serves lists which walls a camera maps onto.
  • fusion: highest_confidence is the safe default; average when two views agree.

This is a clean superset of today’s app: one camera, one wall, one matrix is just the 1×1 case of this file.

Why one declarative file → the whole room is reproducible and diffable, and because the single-wall case is just a subset, no code has to special-case “1 wall” vs “2 walls”.

Step 6 · Calibrate the room

Four calibrations, same routine

With projectors aligned, reuse the interactive corner calibration — run it once per camera/wall pair, pointing at the projected corners you actually see:

  1. Stand in Wall A’s zone. On cam0, point at A’s 4 corners → Space each → saves cam0→A.
  2. Without moving, do cam1 for Wall A → cam1→A.
  3. Step to Wall B’s zone. Calibrate cam1→B, then cam2→B.

Seam check: sweep a hand across the join — the cursor should leave A’s right edge and enter B’s left edge at the same height. If it jumps, re-capture the seam-side corners so the two walls’ edges line up.

Tip: tape floor marks for each wall’s standing zone so calibration and real use share the same vantage point.

cam0→A ✓
cam1→A ✓
cam1→B ✓
cam2→B ✓

cam0→B and cam2→A are intentionally absent — those cameras don’t cover the far wall.

Why per-pair, from the standing spot → the homography encodes one camera’s exact view of one wall, captured from where users actually stand — so calibrating from the real vantage point is what makes pointing land where they expect. The seam check then guarantees the two walls join cleanly.

What will bite you

Failure modes & fixes

Cursor jumps between cameras. Two views of one hand disagree slightly. Fix: hysteresis on “active camera” — keep the current source until its confidence drops clearly below another’s.
Flicker at the seam. Near the join a hand maps in-bounds on both A and B. Fix: seam_margin + handoff hysteresis — commit to the new wall only once the cursor is clearly past the seam.
Occlusion. Person blocks themselves from cam0. Fix: that’s why cam1 exists — fusion falls back to whichever camera still sees the body.
Latency / sync. Per-camera pose at 30 fps; stamp every observation with a time and fuse only recent ones (drop >100 ms old) so a stalled camera can’t freeze a wall.
Multi-user. Raise num_poses and match people across cameras in the room frame before fusing — otherwise two users blur into one cursor.

Why design for these now → every one is a predictable consequence of the physical layout — overlap, occlusion, network jitter. Building the margins and timeouts in up front is far cheaper than diagnosing them live in a room full of people.

Build order

Putting it together

  1. Mount & align the two projectors into one continuous image; lock the seam (butt or edge-blend).
  2. Add a WebSocket “remote pointer” source to the web app; confirm a wall renders from injected {x,y,engaged}.
  3. Stand up the sensing server with one camera → Wall A (parity with today).
  4. Add Wall B client on projector 2 + cam2. Two cameras, two walls, no seam logic yet.
  5. Add cam1 + the fusion / seam handoff (map → gate → handoff → fuse).
  6. Calibrate all four pairs; run the seam check; tune seam_margin & hysteresis.

Hardware checklist

  • 1 host (Apple Silicon is fine) with 3 USB cameras — on separate buses/hubs if you can
  • 2 projectors as an extended desktop, edge-aligned at the seam
  • Even, diffuse lighting (pose hates backlight); keep projector spill off faces
  • Floor marks for each wall’s standing zone

Order is everything: align projectors → calibrate cameras → run.

Why build it incrementally → bringing up one wall, then two, then fusion means each new failure has exactly one possible cause — you always know what just broke.
